# The EFF Just Left X. The Math Was Brutal — and It Should Be a Wake-Up Call

**April 10, 2026**

The Electronic Frontier Foundation announced yesterday that after nearly twenty years on the platform, [they're logging off X for good](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/04/eff-leaving-x). The official reason is refreshingly blunt: the math stopped working.

Here's the number that should stop you mid-scroll: EFF's 1,500 posts in 2025 earned roughly **13 million impressions for the entire year**. By contrast, a single tweet in 2018 would routinely deliver a comparable volume. As EFF's social media manager Kenyatta Thomas put it: *"An X post today receives less than 3% of the views a single tweet delivered seven years ago."*

Read that again. 3%.

---

## This Isn't Nostalgia. It's Forensics.

People have been predicting Twitter's death since approximately 2009, and those people have been wrong in different ways each decade. So it's worth being precise about what's actually happening here versus what's melodrama.

The EFF isn't leaving because Elon Musk hurt their feelings (though they have plenty of substantive criticisms about the platform's deteriorating content moderation and the firing of its human rights team). They're leaving because they ran the numbers and the numbers said: you are wasting your time here.

For an organization whose entire purpose is to reach people who need to know their digital rights, that's a genuinely serious calculation. The EFF has been on the platform since the early days of Twitter — they know how to use it, they know how to grow an audience there, and they chose to stay through multiple cycles of controversy. The fact that even they can't make the engagement math work is telling.

And the broader data corroborates them. According to research tracking X's EU users, [the platform lost 11 million European active users in the second half of 2025 alone](https://www.socialmediatoday.com/news/x-formerly-twitter-sees-decline-eu-users-h2-2025/813715/) — a nearly 15% drop in six months. Time-spent-per-day metrics have slid from an average of 38 minutes pre-Musk to around 24 minutes. Ad revenue has fallen from a peak of roughly $4.5 billion in 2021 to an estimated $2 billion in 2024. These aren't vibes. These are auditable signals.

---

## Why Organic Reach Collapsed (And Why It Won't Come Back)

The destruction of organic reach on X follows a predictable playbook that we've seen before — most thoroughly on Facebook.

When Twitter launched, it was a chronological feed. If someone followed you, they saw what you posted. Engagement was a function of follower count and quality of content. That world no longer exists. After years of algorithmic tweaking, paid promotion incentives, and the dramatic expansion of "For You" feeds serving content from people you don't follow, the connection between your follower count and your actual reach has been severed.

Then there's the signal-to-noise problem. Since 2022, the verification system was dismantled and rebuilt as a pay-to-play blue-check program. Automated and semi-automated accounts have proliferated. Reply sections now routinely feature premium subscribers posting inflammatory content because the algorithm rewards engagement — and outrage drives engagement more reliably than nuance. The result: for accounts publishing substantive, non-inflammatory content (which describes most of EFF's output), the platform has become increasingly hostile.

This is a structural problem, not a temporary rough patch. The incentives are now fully aligned against the kind of discourse that made Twitter worth having in the first place.

---

## "But You're Still on TikTok and Facebook?"

EFF addressed this directly in their post, and it's the most intellectually honest part of their announcement. Yes, they're staying on platforms with arguably worse privacy track records.

Their reasoning: EFF's mission is to reach people who need help protecting their digital rights — and those people are most likely embedded in mainstream platforms, not already living in the fediverse. If your goal is to talk to the choir, Mastodon is great. If your goal is to reach the person who just got a creepy notification from an app they don't remember installing, you need to be where that person actually is.

It's a pragmatic, mission-driven calculation, and it's the kind of reasoning that tends to get drowned out in conversations about platform migration that are largely driven by tribal identity politics. The question isn't "is this platform virtuous?" The question is "does showing up here accomplish anything?"

For X, EFF's answer is now definitively no.

## Where Does That Leave the Rest of Us?

The broader social media fragmentation picture is genuinely complicated. [Bluesky has grown to roughly 40 million registered users](https://sproutsocial.com/insights/bluesky-statistics/), but daily active users hover in the 1.5–3 million range — a fraction of X's remaining base. Mastodon remains ideologically important but small. Threads reached 142 million advertising-reach users but averages about 4 minutes of daily engagement per user. LinkedIn, ironically, has become the most reliably reach-generating platform for a lot of professional and tech-adjacent content creators.

There's no clean migration story here. The fragmentation is real, and different communities have landed in different places for different reasons. Tech discourse has largely moved to Bluesky and, for longer-form takes, back to personal blogs and newsletters. Journalists are split across Bluesky and Threads. Academics and researchers have gravitated toward Mastodon. The net result is that the unified "public square" that Twitter approximated during its peak years (roughly 2012–2020) probably doesn't exist anymore, and might not be rebuilt.

Whether that's good or bad depends enormously on what you wanted the public square for. If you valued it for breaking news and real-time discourse, X still technically serves that function — [its 59.7% news-use rate remains the highest of any social platform](https://onclusive.com/resources/blog/x-twitter-statistics-2026/). If you valued it for building a professional audience or reaching people who care about nuanced topics, the EFF's experience is pretty illustrative of what you're looking at.

---

## The Actual Takeaway

The EFF's departure isn't a bombshell. It's a data point in a long trend that's now hard to ignore.

X is not dying in any dramatic, sudden sense. It still has hundreds of millions of accounts and enormous structural importance as a real-time information network. But it has quietly hollowed out as a place where organizations and individuals can build meaningful, non-polarizing reach. The users who drove substantive discourse have disproportionately left or reduced their activity. What remains is skewed toward the inflammatory and the algorithmic.

If you're a developer, researcher, activist, educator, or creator who built part of your distribution strategy on Twitter between 2010 and 2020 — and you haven't fundamentally revisited that strategy since then — the EFF's announcement is a prompt worth taking seriously. Not necessarily to leave (though maybe), but to run your own version of the math.

What are you actually getting for the time you're spending there? Not in terms of followers or impressions, but in terms of the outcomes you care about — readership, conversation, impact, leads, whatever it is?

If the number you come up with is "less than 3% of what it was in 2018," you have your answer.
