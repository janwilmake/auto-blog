# The Microsoft-OpenAI Divorce Isn't a Divorce. They Killed the Clause That Could Have Burned Everything Down.

*April 28, 2026*

The framing you're seeing everywhere is that OpenAI "won" its freedom from Microsoft. OpenAI can now put its models on AWS, sell to Google Cloud customers, and generally stop being an Azure-only shop. That's true, and it's a significant operational shift. But the headline buries the actual story.

The real victory, shared equally by both companies, is that they finally killed the AGI clause.

## A Contract That Couldn't Work

If you weren't tracking this closely, the AGI clause was a landmine embedded in every version of the Microsoft-OpenAI deal since 2019. The original structure was simple in the way that things are simple before they blow up: Microsoft gets exclusive IP rights and Azure exclusivity *until OpenAI achieves AGI*. At that point, the commercial arrangement largely dissolves — OpenAI's mission is complete, the profit-sharing provisions change, and Microsoft's access rights get murky.

The problem is that nobody ever defined AGI in a way that could survive a legal dispute involving billions of dollars.

The financial definition that eventually leaked — AGI is achieved when OpenAI's systems can generate $100 billion in total profits for early investors — was corporate nonsense dressed up as a technical milestone. It turned an existential question about AI capability into an accounting trigger. [Simon Willison documented the full history](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Apr/27/now-deceased-agi-clause/) of how the clause evolved across every renegotiation, and reading it in sequence is remarkable: each version gets slightly more specific, slightly less ambiguous, and yet somehow still manages to leave room for a nine-figure legal fight.

Last October's deal tried to fix this by routing any AGI declaration through an independent expert panel. Better, but still not good enough. The Reuters Breakingviews analysis from March [said it clearly](https://www.reuters.com/commentary/breakingviews/openais-agi-chase-is-tricky-concept-contract-2026-03-16/): the opacity of the agreement and the huge sums at stake made a contractual dispute "almost inevitable." Microsoft reportedly contemplated legal action as recently as February, when OpenAI's Amazon deal seemed to violate existing exclusivity terms.

As of Monday, April 27, the clause is dead. Revenue share continues through 2030 "independent of OpenAI's technology progress" — that last phrase is doing enormous work. It means: regardless of whether anyone declares AGI, regardless of what capability threshold gets hit, regardless of what Sam Altman tells a CNBC interviewer. The payments run until 2030 and then they stop. That's a contract that can be enforced.

## What Microsoft Actually Got

Coverage has been oddly unkind to Microsoft here. MSFT stock dropped about 1% on the news, which is the market processing "we lost exclusivity" without properly weighting "we eliminated our biggest contractual liability."

Let me be specific about what Microsoft walked away with:

**Microsoft stops paying revenue share to OpenAI.** This is pure upside for Redmond. Last quarter, Microsoft reported making $7.5 billion from its OpenAI investment in a single quarter. That relationship continues. The checks that previously flowed *to* OpenAI from Microsoft's use of their technology? Gone.

**OpenAI keeps paying Microsoft 20% through 2030, now capped.** [CNBC confirmed the 20% figure](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/27/openai-shakes-up-partnership-microsoft-capping-revenue-share-payments.html). On every ChatGPT subscription sold, every API call through Azure, every enterprise deal — Microsoft gets a cut, with a ceiling. The cap is a concession, but the 2030 certainty (previously dependent on AGI timing) is worth more than it looks, especially with OpenAI heading toward an IPO valuing it near $1 trillion.

**Microsoft keeps an IP license through 2032, plus a 27% equity stake.** The license is now non-exclusive, yes. But Microsoft can still build Copilot on OpenAI models, integrate GPT-5.x into Azure products, and generally keep doing everything they've been doing. Meanwhile, every AWS Bedrock deal OpenAI signs, every Google Cloud integration, every new enterprise customer — Microsoft captures 27% of the upside as a shareholder.

**The AGI trigger that could have ended payments and IP rights at any moment is gone.** This is the biggest one. Microsoft's revenue certainty through 2030 is no longer conditional on OpenAI's board not making a unilateral declaration about a concept that nobody can define.

## What OpenAI Actually Got

OpenAI's gains are real too, but they're mostly operational rather than financial.

The immediate problem was the Amazon situation. When OpenAI announced its up-to-$50 billion AWS deal in February, it promised AWS exclusive rights to the Frontier agent-making tool. Microsoft publicly contradicted this, asserting that its existing agreement made Azure the exclusive cloud for all stateless API products, including Frontier. The Financial Times reported Microsoft was considering legal action. [TechCrunch's analysis](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/27/openai-ends-microsoft-legal-peril-over-its-50b-amazon-deal/) is accurate: this deal is first and foremost OpenAI getting out from under a lawsuit that could have derailed both the Amazon deal and its IPO.

Beyond the immediate legal fix, OpenAI now has genuine cloud flexibility. Andy Jassy [celebrated the news on X](https://x.com/ajassy/status/2048806022253609115) immediately — OpenAI's models will appear on AWS Bedrock "in the coming weeks." Google Cloud will likely follow. Anthropic, which just secured $65 billion between Amazon and Google, has been able to sell to multiple clouds for months. OpenAI was the only frontier lab still operating under structural cloud constraints.

For the IPO, this matters enormously. A company selling stock to public markets while a single contractual clause could theoretically void its key partnerships — depending on an AGI declaration that might or might not happen — is not a company that's easy to value. The new deal creates a clean, calendar-based runway that underwriters and institutional investors can actually model.

## The Deeper Pattern

There's something worth noting about how this played out over seven years. The original 2019 deal was genuinely revolutionary — a $1 billion investment in a research org that most people thought was interesting but probably not transformative. The exclusivity terms made sense at the time. Microsoft was taking a massive risk on an unproven organization; getting exclusive commercial rights was reasonable compensation.

Then ChatGPT happened, and those terms became worth hundreds of billions. The clause that was supposed to reward Microsoft for its risk now looked like a permanent structural lock on one of the most valuable technologies ever created. OpenAI's computing demands outgrew what Azure could supply. Competitors emerged. The AGI definition became increasingly absurd as capability improvements became incremental rather than discontinuous.

Both companies spent years renegotiating around the edges — the 2023 extensions, the October 2025 restructuring, the February 2026 joint statement — before finally doing what everyone could see was necessary: replacing an AGI-contingent arrangement with a time-bounded one.

The lesson isn't really about AI. It's about what happens when a contract is built around a variable that can't be measured. You can paper over it for a while, but eventually one side or the other has to call the measurement, and at that point you're in litigation, not partnership. Both companies had a strong incentive to defuse this before it detonated.

They did. The deal isn't a breakup. It's just a contract that might actually work.
